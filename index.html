Plan Design

Plan design is where a wellness program is won or lost, and it is the part of Snout we have spent the most time getting right. As a group you decide how many plans to offer and what goes in each. This page is how our plans work, and the few decisions that are yours to make.

How Snout worksThe group sets the plan structure and guardrails once, and every hospital runs that set. Each clinic still keeps medical autonomy over which of its own codes qualify.

The problem we set out to solve

Wellness plans have long had the same problem. They assume every pet needs the same out-of-the-box care. But every pet is different. Treat them all the same and you get unused benefits, owners who feel they overpaid, and plans that quietly do not renew. Swing the other way and ask the owner to assemble what their pet needs, and that fails too, because the owner is not the one who should be making medical calls. We have spent years on the space between those two extremes, and we think we have landed in the right place.

How our plans work: plan items

The answer is the plan item. Each plan is built from a handful of plan items, categories of care such as exams, vaccines, fecal screening, or bloodwork. Every item carries a dollar cap set during design, and any qualifying service the pet actually needs counts toward it. “Vaccines” covers whatever vaccines this pet needs up to the cap, and “bloodwork” covers the clinic's designated panel. The clinic decides which services qualify, the veterinarian keeps full medical autonomy, and the owner still sees one simple plan. How those services map to each clinic's own codes is its own topic, covered next in Code Mapping & Coverage.

Base plans cover routine wellness. Add-ons let an owner cover the bigger, predictable expenses too, spread across the year. The group decides which to offer, and we recommend all three.

It tends to catch concerns earlier, deflect unnecessary trips, and add value members genuinely feel. The group decides whether to offer it across the group, and we recommend yes.

Who holds the pen

Plans are set, not customized clinic by clinic. The group designs the plans once and approves the structure, and every hospital runs that same set. You hold the pen on design. Pricing is set together and is specific to each clinic, because each one bills its own fees. The clinic keeps medical autonomy over which services qualify, so a veterinarian who wants, say, two fecal screens instead of one can ask, and the group decides whether to allow it. The pattern is consistent: structure is yours, pricing is joint, medical calls stay with the clinic, and anything non-standard is decided at the group level before it reaches a hospital.

For a group that manages pricing centrally, the plan-draft call compresses. Your team pre-curates the plans, we validate the codes and pricing behind them, and the call becomes a short confirmation with the doctors rather than a working session. Doctor review is the longest pole in any launch, so everything pre-aligned up front shortens the whole calendar.

Plan versions, and who keeps their plan

When a member enrolls, they enroll into a specific version of the plan, the one designed and priced at that moment, and they keep it for the full term. If the group later changes a plan, new enrollees get the new version and existing members move into it only at their next renewal. Each version is tracked as its own cohort, so you can always see who is on what. Members are only moved between versions if something needs correcting, and never silently.

Your decisions, in one place

Everything above comes down to a few choices the group makes once:

  • How many plans, and whether to include a senior tier. Recommended: include it.
  • Which add-ons to offer. Recommended: all three.
  • Prevention products: lock to one per category, or leave to clinic discretion.
  • Telemedicine: whether to offer Airvet across the group. Recommended: yes.

And when the group is ready to make this concrete, the fastest step is a working session on one candidate clinic: real invoice data, a real plan draft, walked end to end. It turns every abstract question on this page into a specific answer.

The Medical Perspective

For medical leadership: what the plans cover clinically, where the doctor's judgment sits, and the guardrails that keep the medicine yours. Nothing in this program tells a veterinarian how to practice.

The clinical case

When the cost of a visit stops being the question, owners come in. Members visit far more often (+72% visit frequency), which means problems caught earlier, vaccine series actually completed, prevention actually dispensed, and senior pets getting bloodwork before disease is advanced instead of after. The program's whole job is to remove the financial barrier between the doctor's recommendation and the owner's yes.

The doctor decides the medicine

Plans are built from plan items, and a plan item is a category of care, not a protocol: vaccines, fecal, bloodwork. Any qualifying service counts toward the item, so the doctor chooses lepto and lyme for the farm dog and skips them for the apartment cat, and the plan covers either choice. Nothing in the plan dictates which product, panel, or protocol to use.

There is also no good / better / best menu, because that asks the owner to price the medicine. The doctor makes one recommendation, and the owner says yes or no, the same as any treatment plan today.

When a doctor wants something different

If a veterinarian wants the plan handled differently, say two fecal screens a year instead of one, the request routes to the group. If the group approves, we build it. Autonomy at the exam table, consistency across the group, and nothing decided ad hoc.

By default, a plan is tied to the clinic where the owner enrolled. For a corporate organization that owns more than one location, we can do much more: make a member's plan usable across the group, even when those locations run different practice management systems.

What it unlocks

  • Interchangeable use across locations. A member can use their plan at any participating hospital in the group, not only the one where they enrolled.
  • Works across different PIMS. The group's hospitals do not have to share one system for this to work, which is rare and powerful for a multi-brand or acquisitive group.
  • One membership, one experience. The owner has a single plan and a single place to manage it, no matter which location they visit.

Why it matters for a group

Owners move, travel, or simply prefer a sister location for a given visit. Interchangeable plans mean a member is never turned away or asked to re-enroll, which protects retention and keeps care inside the group rather than leaking to an outside clinic. For an acquisitive group bringing hospitals onto different systems, it also means the wellness program does not fragment location by location.

Why waves

We onboard in waves rather than launching everything at once, and we have found it is simply more successful than taking clinics one at a time. A few reasons:

  • Clinics have a better experience together. Going live alongside similar hospitals on the same timeline means teams are working through the same things at the same moment, not on their own.
  • Faster feedback loops. Each wave surfaces what is working and what is not, quickly, so we learn and iterate before the next wave rather than repeating the same friction.
  • Everyone stays organized. A predictable cadence keeps both your hospitals and our team on the same page, with clear dates and no one underwater.
  • Real attention per hospital. Each clinic gets a focused launch instead of being one of hundreds going live the same day.

The leadership session

We train leaders first, on purpose. The session walks the final plans, answers every question still open from the plan-draft call, and settles how the clinic will run the program: who owns it day to day (the in-clinic champion), how the desk will handle checkout, and what the first weeks should look like. Together we set 7, 30, and 60 day goals scaled to the practice's patient volume, paired with a launch incentive for the team.

One ask we make of every leadership team: tell your staff what is coming, and why the clinic said yes, before the all-team session. Teams that hear it from their own leaders first walk in engaged and ask better questions than rooms hearing everything cold for the first time.

The team session

The all-staff session covers the mechanics in the order the desk meets them: recognizing a member, the two-step checkout, split payments, and where answers live, the cheat sheet, the portal, and member support. Then the client conversation: when to bring plans up, leading with unlimited exams, and word-for-word answers to the questions owners actually ask.

It is built around the questions clinic teams raise everywhere, bundled bloodwork, split payments, what to say at the desk, so most of the room's questions are answered before anyone asks, and anything clinic-specific gets a direct answer on the call.

And because staff sell what they believe in, we close with what the plans do for pets. Real members, real outcomes: one dog on a $42 a month plan received more than $3,500 of care across seven months and came in every single one of them. That is the part teams remember.

Timed to the launch

Team training lands in launch week, with printed materials already in the building, so the energy from the session carries straight to the desk. A clinic should launch the same day it trains. The session ends, the materials are on the counter, and the team starts enrolling that afternoon while everything is fresh. Letting training and go-live drift apart is how launches lose their momentum, so we simply do not separate them.

Two scheduling notes from the groups that do this well. Run your own kickoff first, leadership delivering the why before Snout ever presents, because teams take the program more seriously when it clearly comes from their own organization. And give hospitals about three weeks' notice for training dates. Short-notice invitations do not get attended.

After go-live

  • The CSM checks in against the 7, 30, and 60 day goals and helps course-correct early, while adoption habits are still forming.
  • Refresher and new-hire training are available whenever the clinic wants them.
  • The morning bad-standing email and the portal keep the desk sharp day to day.

PIMS Integration

Snout has spent years building practice management integrations, and today we integrate with roughly 99% of the systems on the market. For any system we integrate with, getting a clinic live takes two steps: setting up the data connection, then adding Snout as a payment type.

How Snout worksThe connection is read-only. We never write back to your system, and staff simply mark Snout as a payment type at checkout.
Read-only, on purpose

This is intentionally a read-only data connection. We never write back to your system or touch your source of truth. We have seen write-back integrations go wrong too many times, so we read only the data we need to run the program, nothing more.

What the connection powers

Getting a clinic live

Code Mapping & Coverage

This is one of the most sophisticated parts of Snout, and there is nothing the group or its hospitals build or maintain. Every clinic is different and uses different codes, new ones and old ones, so the real work is making sense of all of them automatically. Here is how.

Every clinic is different

No two hospitals code the same way. Different systems, different naming, codes added years ago and codes added last week. Rather than ask anyone to clean that up, we read each clinic's codes exactly as they are and do the mapping ourselves.

How mapping works

Using the read-only PIMS connection, we pull the last 12 to 24 months of invoice history and map every single code we see. We do it with our own algorithm, trained on hundreds of thousands of clinic invoices over the years, not an LLM and not a person guessing. Each code is mapped to a specific plan item, exams, vaccines, heartworm test, fecal, or bloodwork, so that whenever any code shows up on an invoice, we already know exactly what it relates to and whether it is covered. Each mapping carries a confidence score, and our vet-experienced admins review anything that does not map cleanly, confirming the high-use ones with the clinic on the plan draft call.

When a code is ambiguous

If a code description is unclear, or a high-use code maps with low confidence, it is never silently guessed. It routes to an admin, and if it is material we ask the clinic directly before finalizing. Low-use, low-ambiguity codes map automatically.

The clinic's raw codesPlan items

Every code a clinic uses, however it is named, maps to the plan item it belongs to. Several codes often roll up into one item.

Bundled and packaged codes

A single code can map to more than one plan item, which is how we handle bundles. These are the exact cases that come up, and how each one resolves:

Worked examples
1
“Exam with Vaccines” (one line, exam plus three vaccines). We ask the clinic what is inside and map the line to each underlying item. Usually it is better unbundled, so each piece is tracked and priced at full rack rate now that Snout covers it.
2
“Puppy Package” (lab plus vaccines plus exam, all in one). Same approach: confirm the contents, map to the items. These are usually heavily discounted, so the clinic is generally better off retiring the package and charging a la carte.
3
A $500 package charge with itemized $0 line items. We map off the package code itself, not the $0 lines. If the clinic prefers, we break it into individually priced items so each one is covered and tracked cleanly.

Secondary codes

We map the most commonly used secondary codes that ride along with a primary one, like a blood draw fee on covered bloodwork or a dispensing fee on a covered parasiticide. As long as the secondary is what happens in the practice today and rides on a covered item, it is covered too.

What gets covered, by code

The rule is simple: every code that belongs to a plan item gets mapped and covered up to its cap. If a plan includes exams, vaccines, heartworm test, fecal, and bloodwork, then every vaccine code, every qualifying fecal code, and so on is covered. Only codes outside the plan's items are not. The clinic receives a full, itemized caps list once plans are finalized, with complete visibility into exactly what is covered, code by code.

Rebates and promotions

Manufacturer rebates and promotions, like Greenline on a covered parasiticide, still benefit the owner. The rebate applies to any non-covered balance on the invoice, and if the invoice is fully covered with no balance to offset, we coordinate with the clinic to put the rebate on the owner's account as a credit, so the value is never lost.

Clinic-side rebate programs are unaffected too. If the clinic earns reporting rebates on specific codes or product usage, Greenline included, nothing changes: it bills exactly as it does today, with no code or pricing changes, and keeps full reporting credit for quantities and eligible items even when the owner is a Snout member. Snout does not interfere with rebate programs or their reporting.

One thing rebates never do is enter plan pricing. They benefit the owner at the invoice, so the plan's economics never depend on a rebate program that could change next year.

Keeping it current

Once plans are built, our admin team owns the upkeep. If a new or changed code appears, for example a clinic switches lab panels, we catch it and assign it to the right item so the mapping stays current. No one at the group or the clinic maintains a code list.

Data schema for your own modeling

So your team can model independently, we share the plan-item structure, the per-code mapping with its caps, and the column layout of the underlying invoice and charge data: column names, formats, and code-list structure. You can run your evaluation against your own warehouse, not just take a finished number from us.

How we turn all of this mapping into the actual plan price is its own topic, covered next in Pricing. Dispute handling, both client-versus-coverage and clinic-versus-reimbursement, is under FAQ & Edge Cases.

Getting a clinic live

Pricing

Plan pricing is done in partnership. We do the heavy lifting with data, and the clinic, or the corporate group, has final approval on everything. The goal underneath all of it is simple: clinics should get paid what they invoice.

How Snout worksPlans are priced per clinic, from that clinic's own historical fees, never a national rate. The group has final approval on every price.

How a plan is priced

A plan price is the cost of the included services divided by twelve, plus an estimate for the exams we expect to cover over the year. We do not ask the clinic to guess at any of it. We use the code mapping and the clinic's own invoice history to hand them what we will charge for each item.

Pricing each item from real data

Because a plan covers all vaccines, not one specific vaccine, we cannot simply pick a price. So we use a weighted average. To price a “dog vaccine,” for example, we look at the actual mix of vaccines dogs received at that clinic over the past 6 to 18 months and compute the weighted-average cost across them. That is the math behind every line item, and we do it for the clinic so no one has to guess.

Why we do it this way

It keeps the plan flexible for whatever a pet actually needs, while making sure the clinic is paid what it would have invoiced. Everyone works hard for their prices and their margins, so using each clinic's own historical data to set every item's price is how we protect that.

Reviewing prices across the group

When we work with a corporate group, we aggregate all of the plan prices before launch and compare each one against that clinic's average client transaction. The point is to make sure the numbers make sense and that no clinic ends up drastically out of line with its peers. Owners in a given area are used to paying a certain amount at their clinic, shaped by the local market and demographics, so plan prices need to sit naturally within that range. We have found that plans priced too high do not sell, and plans priced too low do not sell either, because the clinic's own team does not see the value and stops bringing them up. Getting that balance right takes work, and we do it together with your team.

When your prices change

Prices move. Your codes will get repriced, sometimes once a year on services, more often on products, and sometimes on short notice when the local market shifts. The plan is built to handle that without disrupting members or your team.

Two things always hold. A member who enrolls locks their price for the full 12-month term, so a mid-year code change never touches an existing member. New enrollees are always priced off current data, so once we re-run a plan, the price posted for sign-up reflects your latest fees.

Exam pricing, the most important part

Unlimited office exams are one of the biggest reasons these plans work. Visits are down nationwide and consumer sentiment is low, so owners hesitate to come back through the door. Removing the financial barrier of the exam fee brings them in far more often. At the same time, clinics and their medical teams should always be paid for their time, so how we price exams matters a great deal.

How unlimited exams are priced

We cover every exam type. From a clinic's own data, we see the expected exam spend per patient and express it as a single dollar amount priced into the plan. Say we price in $230 of exams: based on our algorithm, the data, and that clinic's own history, we expect a member to use about $230 of exams in a year. That spans everything, wellness visits, sick exams, urgent care, rechecks, and technician visits, not just wellness. The only things we exclude are overnight hospitalizations and emergencies, with a handful of corner cases we work through during code mapping.

Under the hood

That $230 works out to roughly 2.4 visits a year for the average member. We typically do not surface this figure to owners, it is simply how the plan is priced underneath. Every clinic is different, so we price it to each one.

How we confirm it with the clinic

During code mapping we show the clinic every exam type we see and say, plainly: all of these will be covered except X, are you comfortable with that? If they want an excluded type included, that is fine, it simply moves the price, maybe a couple of dollars a month on the plan, and the clinic or group decides. Nothing is priced without their sign-off.

Starting from a file, before the PIMS is connected

Plan design does not have to wait for the integration. For a group that wants draft plans and a cost model first, we work from a flat file: 12 to 18 months of invoice line-item detail, one row per line item, with date, code or description, price, quantity, and a patient identifier. We map and price from the file exactly as we would from the PIMS, and current prices come from recent transactions rather than a posted catalog, so a January price change surfaces on its own.

The one thing no invoice history reveals is add-on pricing. There is no standard dental price in the data, because every dental differs in time under anesthesia and extractions. So spay and neuter, dental, and parasiticide pricing is confirmed directly through a short form, and for a group, one bulk template your team fills out replaces that step clinic by clinic. Quantity-break products, like a six-dose box priced below six singles, are handled there too, so no blended average sneaks into a plan.

Discounting

Whether clinics can discount plans is a decision for the group, and one we walk through during pricing. Our recommendation is to keep it off: because we price each plan fairly from a clinic's own data, members already get strong value, so layering a discount on top is usually unnecessary and it changes the economics. If a clinic does discount a member invoice, the discounted amount is what pays out, less the same fee on covered items, so the clinic absorbs the cost of the discount. Pricing the plans right from the start is what keeps discounting off the table.

At the clinic

Payments & Receipts

In general we recommend running member invoices through Snout, so staff are not breaking the invoice out and deciding line by line what is covered. Our model is built to make that easy: we charge our normal 5% on covered items but only 2% on non-covered, so a clinic is never penalized for running non-plan items through us.

How Snout worksWe pay the clinic when care is delivered. A covered service pays out the following week, booked in your PIMS like any other payment, so production pay and reporting are unaffected.

Three checkout scenarios

100% covered
Every line item is on plan and within cap. Staff mark the invoice as paid by Snout. The owner owes nothing at the desk. The clinic nets the price minus 5%.
Mixed invoice
Some items on plan, some not, all through Snout. Covered items pay out at 95%, non-covered at 98%. We collect the non-covered portion from the card on file. One clean payout, one place to pay.
Split payment
The owner pays part another way (CareCredit, Scratchpay, cash). Staff take a split payment on the one invoice, charge the other method, and run the rest through Snout. We auto-detect the in-clinic portion. The clinic keeps 100% of that piece.

Snout carries default risk, not the clinic. Collections are handled in house by US-based staff, never sold to an agency. We run smart card retries across different days and times and reach out directly by phone, email, and text. If a member cannot be made current after 30 days they move to default status, which surfaces in the portal and in a morning alert to the clinic. The fix is simply taking a new card on file. A clinic is still paid for services even when a member later defaults, and the agreement includes a per-clinic default cap that exists mainly to protect against fraud.

Clinic Selection

Which clinics you choose, especially in the early waves, is one of the most important decisions in the whole rollout. Snout does everything it can to put the plan in front of pet owners and prompt the conversation, but a wellness program is ultimately a change-management exercise, and it works best when the clinics are genuinely bought in. The first waves set the tone, so we pick deliberately.

What the most successful clinics have in common

  • Bought-in leadership. A tenured practice manager or medical director who actually wants this, with no recent leadership turnover.
  • A strong, stable front desk. Low CSR turnover and a team comfortable talking to clients about care and cost, since enrollment happens at checkout.
  • Healthy doctor count and staffing. Roughly 2 to 5 doctors, fully staffed, with no open DVM seats.
  • Steady volume. Busy enough for consistent foot traffic, roughly 40+ transactions a day.
  • Nothing major in flight. Not mid-renovation, mid-PIMS-switch, a new manager, an ownership change, or another big competing initiative.

How to choose your early waves

Start with hand-raisers

Hospitals that have already said they want wellness plans. Their enthusiasm gets the program off to a fast start.

Pair with strong operators

Steady, well-run clinics with nothing major in flight. They become the reliable proof points the later waves look to.

Keep regions tight

For about 30 clinics, concentrate under two or three regional leaders, not five or six, so everyone stays in sync. Broaden once it is proven.

Over-select by about 15%

Expect roughly 10 to 15% of selected clinics to fall off after the list is set: someone goes on leave, a hospital gets slammed, priorities shift. It is normal, so to land about 30 live clinics, start by picking around 35.

Testing across performance tiers

Some groups deliberately mix the first cohort, a tranche each of higher, middle, and lower performers, to learn where the program works best before a broader rollout. That works well with two guardrails. First, hold every tranche to the same engagement bar: bought-in leadership and a stable front desk matter in every tier, and a struggling clinic that does not want the program will not tell you anything useful about the tier it represents. Second, read results per tranche rather than as one blended number, so a slower tier does not mask what the strong one is proving. We set the reporting up that way from day one.

Where the bottom-line story is biggest

If you want the clearest financial proof from a pilot, lean toward clinics with a lower starting average ticket. They have the most room to grow, and members there tend to spend more per visit and come in more often. A lower starting ticket paired with a manageable client base is the sweet spot: room to lift and room to penetrate.

FAQ & Edge Cases

The questions clinics and owners ask most, and how we handle the messy real-world cases.

Common clinic questions

Is managing this a full-time job?
No. We handle the plan build, the mapping, reconciliation, member support, and upkeep. The clinic practices as it does today and marks Snout as the payment type at checkout.
Do rebate programs like Greenline still work?
Yes, on both sides. An owner-level rebate applied to an invoice credits against any non-plan balance, and if the invoice is fully covered we coordinate with the clinic to credit the owner's account so the value is never lost. Clinic-level reporting rebates are untouched: bill exactly as today, keep full reporting credit, no code or pricing changes.
We already have wellness plans. What happens to them?
We have migrated clinics off effectively every plan provider. We help you switch members over, set a threshold for small balances, credit owed amounts, and let a small long tail run its course.
What if a client stops paying or removes their card?
Snout carries that risk, not the clinic. We run retries and reach out directly. The clinic is still paid for services. If a member defaults, new services require a fresh card before benefits resume.
Will turning it on disrupt the clinic?
No. It shows up as a payment option in the PIMS. Nothing changes in how the team charts, invoices, or reports.

Edge cases

Owner paid upfront and never used services
We handle refunds proactively and fairly, and our member support team owns the conversation, not the clinic.
Member visits another of the group's locations
Membership travels with the pet. A unified brand across the group means a member can use their plan at any participating hospital.

Disputes and uncommon situations

There should rarely be a dispute, but mistakes happen and we own them. There are two kinds, and each has a clear path.

A client says something should be covered that was not (client ↔ Snout ↔ clinic)
When we are unsure about a mapping or coverage, we ask the clinic first. If we got it wrong, the clinic or the member lets us know and we fix it so it is covered correctly going forward. Member support owns the conversation with the owner, so the clinic does not absorb it. If a miss was material enough to change plan pricing, we work out the right time to adjust, and never make a big change without talking it through with the clinic first.
A disagreement on reimbursement rate (clinic ↔ Snout)
This is handled by the caps agreed during plan drafting. We pull the current price of every covered code and set a cap with a small buffer (about 5%). Within the buffer it is covered in full. Over cap on a premium product, the owner pays the difference. Well over cap from a price increase, we cover it in full the first several times, then revisit the cap with the clinic. Caps are agreed and documented up front, so reimbursement is never a surprise.
A code maps incorrectly, or a new code appears
Our admin team owns mapping upkeep. If a clinic switches lab panels or adds a code, we catch it and assign it to the right plan item. If something was mismapped, we correct it and backfill coverage so the clinic is made whole.
A member wants to discount, or pay a covered item another way
Discounting is supported but discouraged, and the clinic bears the cost of any discount. A member can always split payment and pay part by CareCredit, Scratchpay, or cash, and the in-clinic portion carries no Snout fee.
A rebate or promotion lands on a fully covered invoice
The owner still gets the benefit. The rebate applies to any non-covered balance, and if the invoice is fully covered we coordinate with the clinic to credit the owner's account so the value is not lost.
A member is processed through Snout in error, or while overdue
The clinic is still paid. A per-clinic default cap in the agreement exists mainly to protect against fraud. For overdue members, the fix at the desk is taking a fresh card on file, which reinstates them immediately.

What's Covered

A reference for the team on exactly what a plan covers and how the details work. If a client has questions about what is covered, send them to support@snout.com. For the clinic team, your dedicated CSM can help with any questions about plan details.

Unlimited exams

There is no cap on how often the pet comes in, which gives owners real peace of mind. Exam-type coverage is specific to each hospital, but typical coverage ranges from a technician visit up to urgent care and everything in between. The hospital is paid for every exam invoiced, less Snout's 5% fee. Emergency exams and hospitalizations are excluded.

Vaccines

Vaccines are covered on a quantity basis, set to the hospital's recommendations and standard of care. Any vaccine counts toward that quantity, including lifestyle vaccines like Lyme or influenza, which are included in most plans. Snout covers vaccines as they come through on invoices, up to the plan's quantity, and where applicable covers the most expensive vaccines on an invoice, leaving the least expensive for the owner. A combination vaccine such as DAPP and Lepto may count as two when its price is roughly twice that of a single vaccine. Any vaccines beyond the quantity are the owner's to pay.

Bloodwork and routine testing

Plans typically include the yearly testing your hospital recommends, such as fecal, heartworm, and FeLV/FIV, and most adult plans include wellness bloodwork. If a plan uses a bundled panel (for example CBC, chemistry, heartworm test, and fecal together), all components must be invoiced together as that bundled panel. Stand-alone testing cannot be covered when the plan includes a bundled panel.

Add-ons

  • Spay / neuter. Priced by species, sex, and weight, including pre-op bloodwork. Snout covers up to the priced amount, the owner pays any difference for a higher-cost procedure.
  • Dental. Priced for a routine stage 1–2 cleaning: prophy, anesthesia, IV catheter and fluids, and full-mouth radiographs. Not covered: extractions, extended anesthesia, nerve blocks, take-home pain meds, or antibiotics. If worse disease is found under anesthesia, Snout covers up to the routine cleaning cost and the owner pays the rest.
  • Prevention. A bundled heartworm plus flea and tick add-on, the product flexible to the practice's preference, dispensed all at once or one at a time.

Caps, taxes, rebates, and price changes

  • Caps. Each plan item has a cap, the most Snout will cover for it, set slightly above current pricing as a buffer. Most items are capped about 5% above pricing, and procedures are typically capped $25 above. You receive the full caps list when plans are finalized. If something exceeds its cap, the overage is a vet write-off and the client is never charged.
  • Taxes. The owner's responsibility, charged to their card on file or collected in clinic by split payment.
  • Rebates. Apply only when there is a remaining balance after Snout's coverage, and the balance must be at least the rebate amount for it to apply in full.
  • Price changes. Enrolled members lock their pricing for the full 12-month term. New enrollees get current pricing, and existing members renew into updated pricing with 30 days' notice.

No. Snout wellness plans are not insurance. They are preventive-care plans that make routine services more affordable through monthly payments.

Enrollment

How do pet owners enroll?
By the clinic's QR code or website link. It takes under two minutes.
Can clinic staff enroll on behalf of a pet owner?
Yes, as long as the owner is present. You will need their payment information and billing address, and they must agree to the Snout Membership Agreement at checkout.
What if a pet owner's account isn't found?
They can still enroll by entering their name and email. This can happen if their contact info is missing or was recently updated.

Billing & charging members

How does billing work for clients?
The card on file with Snout is charged once a month for the membership fee. When a member visits, plan-covered items are paid by Snout on the owner's behalf. For anything not in the plan, Snout charges the card on file within 48 hours, and the member gets a detailed emailed receipt after each visit.
How does staff charge a Snout member at checkout?
1. Create the invoice your usual way. 2. Select “Snout” as the payment method. 3. That's it, you do not charge the pet owner. Let them know Snout will charge the card on file within 48 hours for any non-covered items.
A member was checked out without selecting Snout. What now?
Refund the client for those services and resubmit the invoice with Snout selected. If the owner notices first, they can contact Snout and the team coordinates. The clinic is made whole either way.
An account is in bad standing at checkout. What do I do?
Ask for an updated card and enter it on their portal page, which restores good standing immediately. If they decline, charge them in clinic for that visit as you would a non-member. To view the overdue amount, open the owner's account in the portal, scroll to the amount shown in red, and select “view” or “send.”

Plan management

How do unlimited exams work?
They cover both wellness and sick visits, fully paid by Snout on the owner's behalf. Emergency exams and hospitalizations are not included.
How do I verify a member's plan status?
Search the owner's name in the portal: green is good standing, red is bad standing, grey is inactive.
A member disputes whether something is covered.
Send them to Snout's member support team at support@snout.com. The clinic should not absorb the back-and-forth, and every member receipt includes a direct way to reach Snout.
An owner asks if a specific service is covered.
Check their plan in the portal, or send them to Snout support. Covered items always count up to the plan's quantity and caps.
Can a plan be transferred to a different pet?
No. Plans are specific to the individual pet enrolled.
Do plans auto-renew?
Yes, after 12 months. Clients get a renewal email 30 days prior, and can reply to it to adjust their plan selection.

Cancellations

What is the cancellation policy?
Plans are a 12-month commitment. Cancellations are permitted for specific circumstances: a pet passing, rehoming, moving, or changing clinics. A $25 cancellation fee may apply. The owner pays for services used minus monthly fees already paid, and Snout handles the whole process.
What if a pet passes away?
Snout calculates any remaining balance based on services used versus payments made. Monthly charges continue until the balance is paid, then the plan is cancelled. Direct the owner to support@snout.com or (832) 263-3774.
